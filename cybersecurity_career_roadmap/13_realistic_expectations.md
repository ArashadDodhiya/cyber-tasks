## ⏱️ Realistic Timeline for First Cybersecurity Job

### Based on your starting position (dev background + 3-4 months learning + no certs + no experience):

| Scenario | Timeline | Probability | Conditions |
|----------|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Best case** | 6-8 months | 15-20% | You nail a practical interview, get referred, or find a startup willing to take a chance |

| Certifications | eJPT by Month 6 = faster | No cert by Month 9 = slower |

| Networking | Active community presence = faster | Zero connections = slower |
| Bug bounty findings | Even 1 valid finding = much faster | No findings = neutral |
| Market timing | Q1/Q3 hiring seasons = faster | Q4 (budget freeze) = slower |
| Referrals | Having a referral = 5x faster | Cold applying only = slower |

## 🔮 What Success Looks Like at Each Stage

### Month 6 (Minimum Viable Professional)
- You can pentest a web application end-to-end and write a report
- You have a basic but functional portfolio
- You've started applying for jobs
- You have one certification (or it's scheduled)

- You've applied to 100+ jobs
- You've had 5-10 interviews
- Your portfolio showcases 5+ projects
- You have at least 1 certification
- You can confidently explain your methodology in an interview

### Month 18 (If Needed — Not a Failure)
- You've expanded your job search geographically
- You've considered stepping-stone roles (SOC)
- Your skills are now intermediate-advanced
- You may have OSCP or CRTP
- You will get a job. It's a matter of when, not if.

### Month 24 (In Your First Job)
- You're 6-12 months into your first security role
- You're learning how the industry actually works
- You're building real professional experience
- Your salary will jump significantly at your next move
- You're on track for mid-level roles

## 🔑 The Final Truth

**Cybersecurity is one of the few fields where self-taught professionals can genuinely compete with formally trained ones.** The field values what you can DO over where you studied.

But here's the catch: **you have to actually DO things, not just study them.**

Every lab you solve, every tool you build, every bug you find, every post you write — it all compounds. The first 6 months feel like nothing is happening. Then suddenly, opportunities start appearing.

**Your developer background is not a weakness. It's a weapon.** Most pentesters wish they could code. Most developers don't understand security. You're building both.

**The question isn't whether you can break into cybersecurity. It's whether you'll stay consistent long enough for the compound effect to kick in.**

Based on your existing work (100+ docs, home lab, AD environment, structured learning), my honest assessment is:

> **If you follow the balanced roadmap consistently for 12-14 months, you have a 70-80% chance of landing a cybersecurity job with a starting salary of ₹4-7 LPA. If you're flexible on location and role, that probability rises to 85%+.**

That's not a guarantee. That's a realistic probability based on the Indian market. The 15-20% failure case is usually caused by inconsistency, geographic inflexibility, or unrealistic salary expectations — all of which are within YOUR control.
